# Epic: The Giant Music Box

Created: 2025-12-20

---

## Vision Statement

A narrative puzzle game about a programmable music box built with love that fundamentally misses the point of connection. The player explores, solves mechanical puzzles, and discovers a tragic story—but completing it requires releasing judgment and offering presence.

---

## The Story

### The Characters

**Truman**
- A craftsman, deeply loving in his way
- Shy about dancing—stumbled so badly Judith "let him off the hook" out of embarrassment
- Emotionally avoidant, hides behind grand gestures
- His love language is making things, solving problems with his hands
- Not a villain—genuinely trying, genuinely failing

**Judith**
- An exceptional dancer with real grace
- Gave up dancing to become a sentinel
- Loved dancing because it was *social*—being seen, having a partner, performance
- Worldly, with vivid fantasies (Admiral Wesson) she acts out in imagination
- Not a villain—lonely, grieving, finding escape where she can

**The Tragedy**
They loved each other. Neither could bridge the gap. Truman built a private ballroom for them to dance alone—missing entirely that she loved dancing because it was public, witnessed, shared. His gift was devotion made mechanical: beautiful, impressive, hollow at the center.

Her reaction: "She is almost dismissive. But it does affect her."

### The Setting

An abandoned grand ballroom on Athos Island, 10+ years post-collapse.

**The crucial reframe:** This was Judith's room first. "A grand ornamented room, in her style, not his; a place where she can do the thing she loves, and thought she had given up."

When they arrived, she saw a ballroom—parties, guests, dancing, being seen. Truman saw a space for his solution. By the time the music box was finished, the ballroom was gone.

**He colonized her dream with his gift.**

## The Artifact

### The Music Box

Not a machine you stand beside—a structure you stand *inside*. The dancers are enclosed within the mechanism.

**The structure:**
- An open skeletal frame, 12-15 feet diameter, 10+ feet tall
- Like a giant birdcage, a brass gazebo, an oversized four-poster bed frame
- The "bars" are functional: strings, tuned metal bars, pipes
- The programming drum mounted overhead like a crown
- Percussion at the base around the perimeter
- The center is open—a dance floor surrounded by music

**The effect (when working):**
When the drum turns, the mechanism plays *around* the dancers. They're standing inside the music. Surrounded. Enclosed in a private concert for two.

**The thematic weight:**
Truman built a cage of music for them to dance in. Private. Enclosed. Hidden from the world. But Judith loved dancing because it was social—being seen, having an audience. He built her a beautiful prison.

## Core Loop

```
FIX (with watch) → DEPLETE → CHARGE (at fountain) → EXPLORE (without watch) → repeat
     ↓                              ↓
  Truman mode                   Understanding mode
  Doing, solving                Being, seeing
  The mechanism                 The story
```

1. **Fix** — Use the watch to repair music box components
2. **Deplete** — Watch runs out of charge
3. **Charge** — Return watch to fountain, wait
4. **Explore** — While waiting, discover the story
5. **Repeat** — Until everything is fixed... but it still won't play together

## The Mechanics

### The Watch

- Charges at the fountain (the source)
- Enables repairs at the music box
- Requires sincerity to function (world lore)
- Creates the rhythm: fix, then wait, then fix

**What the watch represents:**
- Truman's mode: solving, doing, making
- The tool you rely on
- The thing you must eventually let go

### The Two Pedestals

| Location | Purpose | Metaphorical weight |
|----------|---------|---------------------|
| **Fountain** | Charges the watch | The source, waiting, Judith-time |
| **Music Box** (outside) | Powers repairs | The work, doing, Truman-time |

The player shuttles between them. The habit forms.

### The Door

The music box has a door. It stays closed during repairs.

**Why it stays closed:**
- Mechanisms are exposed inside (safety)
- Repairs need the door sealed to "set"
- The watch works better with containment
- Some practical reason that makes sense

**What this creates:**
You work from outside, looking through the cage bars. The interior is visible but not entered. By the end, you've forgotten the inside is a place you can be.

The final moment asks you to remember: the door opens. You can go in.

### The Curtains

Heavy curtains cover the tall windows. They're closed when you arrive.

**What this hides:**
With curtains drawn, the room is dim. You see:
- A workbench (his desk)
- Tools, mechanisms, equipment
- The strange metal structure in the center
- Dust everywhere

**What you think at first:** "This is a workshop. Some kind of inventor's studio."

The room *lies to you* from the start.

**Why you open them:**
A repair requires it—something practical:
- A mechanism needs natural light to calibrate
- You need to see something outside (the fountain?)
- Dust obscures a detail; sunlight makes it visible
- The watch charges better near light

It's not a revelation moment. Just: "I need more light for this repair."

**What happens when you open them:**
Light floods in. The room transforms:
- These aren't workshop windows—they're ballroom windows (tall, elegant, meant for guests to see in)
- The proportions suddenly make sense—this is a space for dancing
- The floor catches the light—parquet, a dance floor
- Ornate moldings, ceiling height, architecture—all revealed
- The music box looks different: not a machine in a workshop, but an intrusion in a ballroom

**What you think:** "Wait. This isn't a workshop. This is... something else."

**When this happens:**
The curtains open mid-game—after you've formed "Team Truman" but before you find Judith's hopeful letter. The room starts to look wrong before you understand why. The letter later explains what the light already showed you.

**The layered reveal:**
1. Curtains closed → "This is a workshop"
2. Curtains open → "This architecture is strange for a workshop..."
3. Her letter → "Oh. It was a ballroom. He made it into this."

## The Sympathy Arc (Reversed)

### The Player's Journey Through Judgment

| Phase | What you find | Player thinks |
|-------|---------------|---------------|
| **Early** | Truman's desk, his letters about building, his care | "He really tried" |
| **Early-mid** | Evidence of Judith's fantasies, her distance | "She didn't appreciate him" |
| **Mid** | The room starts to look different... | (unease) |
| **Late** | **The early letter. Her hope. The ballroom.** | "Oh no. I misjudged her." |
| **End** | Both stories held together | "Neither was wrong. Both were stuck." |

### The Reversal

**Early game:** You find evidence that makes Judith easy to dismiss.
- Her fantasy life (Admiral Wesson letters)
- Her distance and dismissiveness (from Truman's perspective)
- The unopened dance shoes—she didn't even try
- Player forms "Team Truman"

**Late game:** You find her early letter.
- Written when they first arrived, full of hope
- She describes the ballroom—*her* ballroom—parties, dancing, guests
- She speaks of Truman with love
- **The room transforms in the player's perception**

**The reframe:**
- The fantasy wasn't frivolous—it was escape from grief
- The distance wasn't ingratitude—it was watching her dream get replaced
- The shoes weren't rejection—she couldn't dance in this space anymore
- The dismissiveness wasn't cruelty—it was self-protection

**The player's shame:**
"I judged her. I sided with him because his evidence came first. I did exactly what the game is about—I judged without seeing."

## The Twist

### What Doesn't Work

You fix everything. Every mechanism repaired. You try to make it play.

**It doesn't work.**

Each piece functions, but they won't play together. The machine is repaired but not alive.

You try moving the watch between pedestals. Neither works fully.

### What Does Work

1. **Leave the watch at the fountain** — Return it to the source. Stop fixing.
2. **Remember the door opens** — The forgotten option.
3. **Enter the music box alone** — Bring just yourself, no tool.
4. **Be present** — Not solving, not fleeing. Just there.

### Why This Works Thematically

- Leaving the watch = releasing Truman's trap (fixing as the answer)
- Entering the music box = releasing Judith's trap (distance as protection)
- You do both: leave his tool, step into his gift
- You don't choose a side—you complete the circuit neither could complete alone

**The power flows:** Fountain → Watch (held there) → You (inside the music box) → The Music

You replace the pedestal with your presence.

## The Room as Palimpsest

Once the player knows it was a ballroom, they can't un-see it:

| Element | Curtains closed | Curtains open | After the letter |
|---------|-----------------|---------------|------------------|
| The room | A dim workshop | Something grander... | Her ballroom, colonized |
| Tall windows | Hidden | Elegant, meant for guests | Where people would have watched |
| The floor | Dusty surface | Parquet, catches light | A dance floor, unused |
| The music box | His machine | Looks out of place here | What replaced her vision |
| His desk | His workspace | Incongruous in this room | An intrusion into her space |
| The empty corners | Just shadows | Strangely formal | Where people were supposed to be |
| The proportions | Can't tell | This is meant for dancing | Ballroom architecture, taken |

The reveal is layered: first physical (light), then emotional (understanding).

## The Ending

The music plays.

Not because you fixed it—you did that already.
Not because you chose the right side—there isn't one.

The music plays because someone finally:
- Did all the work (honored Truman's effort)
- Saw all the loss (honored Judith's grief)
- Let go of the tool (stopped fixing)
- Entered the space (stopped fleeing)
- Was present (did what neither of them could do)

**It's an elegy. A completion. A witness.**

Not: "You were smarter than them."
Yes: "You stayed long enough to hear it."

## Thematic Core

### What This Game Is About

You can't connect until you're present as yourself.

But first: you can't be present until you stop judging.

The game makes you judge (Judith seems ungrateful) then corrects you (she was grieving). It trains you to fix (Truman mode) then asks you to stop (the twist). It teaches you habits (door closed, watch in hand) then asks you to break them.

**The lesson:**
- Truman wasn't wrong to build—he was wrong to *only* build
- Judith wasn't wrong to grieve—she was wrong to *only* flee
- The player must do both: honor the work, offer the presence
- Neither verdict is the answer

### The Gap

Neither person was a villain. The tragedy is the space between them that neither could cross.

The music box is a monument to that gap. Impressive. Beautiful. Empty.

Until someone stays.
